What’s a Sonata?



The word “sonata” essentially means “sound piece” & originally referred to anything that was not sung.  Later it came to mean a piece, or group of them, played on a single instrument (e.g. keyboard) or a solo instrument (e.g. violin) with accompaniment.  Scarlatti wrote his 500 or so sonatas as exercises; they are nonetheless very original & quite different from the usual baroque era fare.

All this—apparently—is entirely apart from what is known as “sonata form”, which was widely employed in many types of composition: solo, chamber, or orchestral.  Sonata form was a very old, hoary & venerable musical packaging schema, & composers were slow to abandon it.   At its most basic, it is this:

A
repeat A
B
repeat B
(rarely) Coda

In a major key A almost always modulates to the dominant, or Vth: B then starts from there (usually) & roams back to the home key.  A comprises up to 1/2 of the piece & B is the rest.  B is often longer, because it has a development section exploring the A themes in new ways, &/or introducing new material, before recapitulating the opening themes.  In a minor key, a handful of different modulation patterns are available.

As time passed, it became more & more common to forgo repetition of the B section.

In fine, a sonata is a solo-type work with 1 or 2 instruments; sonata form is a style of presentation.  Many sections of the sonatas on this site are in sonata form, though not all; the form itself is used in all kinds of classical music, including some, such as symphonies, that may have no solo passages.

If you actually understand this give yourself 3—yes, three—gold stars.
